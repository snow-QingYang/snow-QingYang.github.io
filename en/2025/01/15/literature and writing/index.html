A Little Reflection on Writing

A few days ago, I came across a reader asking a blogger how to improve their writing. The blogger gave a simple, unpretentious answer: write 1,000 words every day.

At first glance, this advice seems ordinary and easy to follow. But in truth, the more straightforward the principle, the more profound it often is. As the saying goes, “A great sword has no edge; true mastery hides its brilliance.” I believe that in addition to writing a certain amount every day, it’s equally important to read meaningful texts. Only then can one gain deeper insights and emotional resonance.

I suddenly felt inspired to write this essay on literature after reading a Zhihu post the other day. It offered a reflection on literary writing that I truly agreed with. One particular passage stood out:

Plain description is, and will always be, the highest form of writing. Mindless use of rhetoric is the lowest.
For example, Yu Hua once wrote:
“I never took the initiative to speak to her, but I happily basked in her presence and aura. It was a joy I kept hidden in my heart, known to no one—not even her. I looked at her with a normal gaze, listened carefully to what she said, and responded accordingly. I savored these moments, moments where I could openly admire her beautiful face.”

Beautiful? Not really.

But doesn’t this passage bring you back to your forever unreachable 9th grade classroom? That moment when the girl in the front row turned around, her hair brushing your cheek, and you caught the scent of her shampoo. She handed you a notebook, smiled, and you smiled back like fools for a long while. Then she blushed, covered her mouth, and turned away. You sat there, dazed, holding the notebook as time seemed to slow to a crawl…

That is literature. It captures human emotions. From just a few lines, it evokes something heavy and profound.

Literature preserves joy, sorrow, bitterness, and happiness—allowing readers to relive their own and others’ lives through words. That’s the true magic of writing.

Not piling dozens of adjectives to describe how beautiful the cherry blossoms are.

Reading this struck a chord deep within me. The heart of literature lies in capturing genuine human emotion. Back in middle and high school, teachers often emphasized the importance of “real emotion” in writing, in addition to the basic skills. But unfortunately, most of us didn’t fully grasp what that meant. Especially with the exam system gradually favoring structured, formulaic essays, students were more likely to chase “technique” in the wrong direction…

I still remember my own experience taking the last traditional version of the Jiangsu college entrance exam in 2020. The essay prompt was about “meeting kindred spirits in the age of intelligent internet” (most wrote about filter bubbles and independent thinking, of course). But having read too many novels at the time, I didn’t feel like writing another argument. So I let my emotions guide me and wrote a narrative essay instead.

Looking back, it was probably one of the boldest things I did at a “critical moment” in life. Surprisingly, it turned out pretty well—I scored 123 out of 140. Not bad, huh? If only I could read that essay again… but sadly, national exams don’t let you see your paper.

Now that I’m in university, though life is busy with classes and research, I’ve held on to the habit of reading fiction and writing (and yes, visual novels count as literature too!). Since I want to continue nurturing this love for words, maybe it’s time to take that blogger’s advice seriously—write a little each day, read a little each day. As the saying goes, “A journey of a thousand miles begins with a single step. A mighty river begins as a trickle.”

To everyone who loves literature—let’s keep going, together.

— Written on January 15, 2025, in snowy Toronto.
